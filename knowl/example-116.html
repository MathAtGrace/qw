Example 6.2.3.

Here is a two-party election with preferences shown below. It is a close race, but candidate B would win.
Number of voters 96 100
1st choice A B
2nd choice B A
Suppose a third candidate, C, entered the race, and a segment of voters sincerely want to vote for that third candidate. Here is the new preference schedule.
Number of voters 96 90 10
1st choice A B C
2nd choice B A B
3rd choice C C A
The ten people who prefer candidate C are in a bind because if they vote for C, then A will win, but they prefer B over A. It is likely that they will vote for B, their second choice, to keep A from winning.
